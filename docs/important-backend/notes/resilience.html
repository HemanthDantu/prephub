Start Here — Services Will Fail. Plan for It.

Understanding why resilience is not optional in microservices.

🧮 The Maths of Microservice Availability

Imagine each of your 10 microservices has 99.9% uptime — that is only 8.7 hours of downtime per year. Sounds great. But here is the problem:

If a user request must pass through all 10 services, the overall availability is:

99.9% × 99.9% × ... (10 times) = 99.0%

That is 87 hours of downtime per year — over 3 full days. And that is with excellent individual service availability. With 20 services: 98% availability — over 7 days of downtime per year.

Services WILL go down. Network calls WILL fail. The question is not whether your system experiences failure — it is how gracefully it degrades when failure happens. A well-designed system with poor resilience fails completely. A well-designed system with good resilience degrades partially and recovers automatically.

The Five Layers of Defence — Like Wearing Body Armour

Think of resilience as layers of protection. Each layer catches failures that slipped through the previous one. You do not rely on any single layer — you stack them.

Layer 1 — Timeout: Do not wait forever for a response.
Layer 2 — Retry: Try again on transient failures.
Layer 3 — Circuit Breaker: Stop trying when too many failures happen.
Layer 4 — Fallback: Return something useful when the service is unavailable.
Layer 5 — Bulkhead: Isolate failures so they do not spread.

1
Timeout — The Simplest Defence
If a downstream service does not respond within X seconds, give up and release the thread. Without a timeout, your thread waits forever — a blocked thread that never returns is how cascading failures start. Always set both a connect timeout (how long to wait for the connection) and a read timeout (how long to wait for the response once connected).
2
Retry — Handle Transient Failures
Many failures are temporary: a brief network blip, a GC pause on the server, a momentary overload. Retrying after a short wait often succeeds. But retry only on safe, transient errors — never blindly retry all failures.
3
Circuit Breaker — Stop Hitting a Dead Service
If retries keep failing, the service is not just having a blip — it is truly down. Stop calling it. Return the fallback immediately without wasting time on a call that will fail anyway. Give the service time to recover. Try again after a wait period.
4
Fallback — Return Something Useful
When the circuit is open, return a meaningful response instead of an error. Stale cached data, a safe default, or a friendly degraded experience is infinitely better than a 500 error. The fallback should be designed deliberately — what is the least bad experience?
5
Bulkhead — Contain the Blast Radius
Separate thread pools per downstream dependency. If InventoryService is slow and fills its thread pool, PaymentService calls — in a different thread pool — are completely unaffected. Named after ship bulkheads: a hole in one compartment does not sink the ship.
⚡
Circuit Breaker — Deep Dive

The most important resilience pattern. Understand the three states and every transition.

Formal Definition

A Circuit Breaker is a resilience pattern that monitors calls to a downstream service and automatically stops making those calls when too many failures occur, returning a fallback response instead. After a wait period, it allows a limited number of probe requests to test whether the service has recovered.

Named after the electrical circuit breaker in your home — when there is too much current (too many failures), the breaker trips to protect the rest of the circuit (your system).

The Electrical Circuit Breaker Analogy

In your home, an electrical circuit breaker monitors current flow. Under normal conditions (CLOSED), electricity flows normally. When a fault causes too much current (too many failures), the breaker trips (OPEN) — it cuts the circuit to protect your appliances. You cannot turn it back on immediately — you must wait, find the fault, fix it, then manually reset the breaker (HALF-OPEN — test if it is fixed).

The software circuit breaker works identically. Normal calls → CLOSED. Too many failures → OPEN (stop calling, return fallback). After wait period → HALF-OPEN (try one probe call). Probe succeeds → CLOSED. Probe fails → back to OPEN.

🟢
CLOSED
Normal operation. All calls go through to the downstream service. Every call result (success or failure) is recorded in a sliding window. When the failure rate exceeds the threshold — the circuit trips to OPEN.
🔴
OPEN
Circuit is tripped. All calls immediately return the fallback — NO actual call is made to the downstream service. Protects the failing service from being hammered. After the wait duration expires, moves to HALF-OPEN.
🟡
HALF-OPEN
Probe state. A limited number of test calls are allowed through. If enough succeed — service has recovered, move to CLOSED. If too many fail — service is still down, move back to OPEN.
CLOSED → OPEN When failure rate exceeds threshold (e.g. 50% of last 10 calls failed)
OPEN → HALF-OPEN After waitDurationInOpenState expires (e.g. 60 seconds have passed)
HALF-OPEN → CLOSED When probe calls succeed (e.g. 2 out of 3 probe calls succeeded)
HALF-OPEN → OPEN When probe calls still fail (service has not recovered yet)
Step-by-Step Walkthrough — PaymentService Goes Down

Normal operation (CLOSED): Your service calls PaymentService. Every call result is recorded in a sliding window of the last 10 calls.

Calls so far: OK, OK, FAIL, OK, FAIL, FAIL, FAIL, FAIL, FAIL, FAIL → 8 failures in 10 calls = 80% failure rate → threshold was 50% → CIRCUIT TRIPS TO OPEN

OPEN state (60 seconds): Every call to PaymentService immediately returns the fallback response — no actual network call is made. PaymentService gets zero traffic and has 60 seconds to recover. Your threads are freed immediately instead of waiting for timeouts.

HALF-OPEN probe: After 60 seconds, 3 probe calls are allowed through. Probe 1: OK. Probe 2: OK. Probe 3: OK. All 3 succeed → CIRCUIT CLOSES — service recovered. Normal traffic resumes.

If probes fail: PaymentService still down. Circuit goes back to OPEN for another 60 seconds. Repeats until the service recovers.

📋 What Should Your Fallback Actually Return?

This is the question from your notes: "What fallback response are we sending? What does the user see? What should the user do?"

A fallback should never be a 500 error. A 500 tells the user "something is broken" with no further guidance. Design your fallback to give the best possible experience given the circumstances.

Retry Strategy — How to Retry Safely

Not all retries are equal. A bad retry strategy can make failures worse.

🤔 What Should and Should Not Be Retried

Retrying the wrong operations causes more harm than not retrying at all.

⏱️ Exponential Backoff — Wait Longer Between Each Retry

Do not retry immediately. Wait before retrying, and wait longer each time. If the service is overloaded, hammering it with immediate retries makes it worse, not better.

Exponential backoff doubles the wait time after each failure:

  • Second attempt (retry 1): wait 500ms
  • Third attempt (retry 2): wait 1000ms

⚡ Jitter — Why You Must Add Randomness to Backoff

The thundering herd problem: Your payment gateway goes down at 2pm. You have 500 services all retrying with the same exponential backoff schedule. At exactly t+500ms, all 500 retry simultaneously. The recovering gateway sees a spike of 500 requests and crashes again. At t+1000ms, another 500-request spike. The service cannot recover because it keeps getting overwhelmed at every retry interval.

The fix — jitter (randomness): Instead of waiting exactly 500ms, each client waits 500ms ± random(0–250ms). The 500 clients spread their retries across a 500ms window instead of hitting simultaneously. The recovering service sees ~10 requests per 10ms instead of 500 at once. It recovers smoothly.

Amazon published a paper on this specifically — their retry library has jitter built in by default. Every serious retry implementation must include jitter.

Idempotency — Retry Safely Without Creating Duplicates

One of the most important concepts for safe distributed systems. Understand this deeply.

Formal Definition

An operation is idempotent if performing it multiple times produces the exact same result as performing it once. No matter how many times you repeat the operation, the outcome is identical after the first execution.

In a distributed system where network failures can cause uncertainty about whether a request was received and processed, idempotency is what makes retries safe.

🤔 The Problem Idempotency Solves — The Double Charge Nightmare

You are checking out. Your app sends a payment request. The payment service receives it, charges your card successfully, and sends back "200 OK". But your network drops at that exact moment — you never receive the 200. Your app sees a timeout exception and retries. The payment service receives a second request — and charges your card again.

You have been charged twice for one order. No error was raised. No duplicate flag was set. This is a real problem and it happens in production without proper idempotency handling.

HTTP Method / OperationIdempotent?Safe to Retry?Why
GET /orders/123✅ Yes✅ YesReading never changes state. Always returns the same data.
PUT /orders/123 (full replace)✅ Yes✅ YesReplacing with the same data twice gives the same result.
DELETE /orders/123✅ Yes✅ YesDeleting an already-deleted resource returns 404, not a new deletion.
POST /orders (create)❌ No⚠️ Only with Idempotency-KeyEach POST creates a new order. Retry = duplicate order.
POST /payments (charge card)❌ No⚠️ Only with Idempotency-KeyEach POST charges the card. Retry = double charge.
PATCH /orders/123/count +1❌ No❌ Never without keyAdding +1 twice gives count+2. Relative changes are dangerous.
⚙️ How the Idempotency-Key Pattern Works

Before making any non-idempotent POST request, the client generates a UUID. This UUID is the idempotency key. It is generated once per user action — before the first attempt. The same key is sent on every retry.

On the server, before processing the request, check if this key has been seen before. If yes — return the stored result without re-processing. If no — process it, store the result with the key, return the result.

This way: the first request processes and stores the result. All retries find the stored result and return it immediately. The operation only executes once. The client gets the same response every time.

// CLIENT — generate key once before first attempt, reuse on retries public PaymentResponse chargeWithIdempotency(PaymentRequest request) { // Generated ONCE per user action — same key on every retry String idempotencyKey = UUID.randomUUID().toString(); int maxAttempts = 3; for (int attempt = 1; attempt <= maxAttempts; attempt++) { try { return paymentClient.charge(request, idempotencyKey); // same key } catch (TransientException e) { if (attempt == maxAttempts) throw e; Thread.sleep((long) Math.pow(2, attempt) * 500); // backoff } } } // SERVER — check key before processing @PostMapping("/api/payments") public ResponseEntity<PaymentResponse> charge( @RequestHeader("Idempotency-Key") String key, @RequestBody PaymentRequest request) { // Step 1: check if we have already processed this key Optional<PaymentResponse> cached = idempotencyStore.get(key); if (cached.isPresent()) { log.info("Duplicate request detected for key {}, returning cached response", key); return ResponseEntity.ok(cached.get()); // exact same response } // Step 2: process for the first time PaymentResponse result = stripeService.charge(request); // Step 3: store result with key for 24 hours idempotencyStore.save(key, result, Duration.ofHours(24)); return ResponseEntity.ok(result); } // Idempotency Store — Redis implementation @Component public class RedisIdempotencyStore { public Optional<PaymentResponse> get(String key) { String stored = redisTemplate.opsForValue().get("idempotency:" + key); return Optional.ofNullable(stored) .map(s -> objectMapper.readValue(s, PaymentResponse.class)); } public void save(String key, PaymentResponse response, Duration ttl) { String json = objectMapper.writeValueAsString(response); redisTemplate.opsForValue().set("idempotency:" + key, json, ttl); } }
Real World — Stripe's Idempotency Implementation

Stripe is the gold standard for idempotency. Every POST to Stripe's API accepts an Idempotency-Key header. Stripe stores this key with the result. If the same key arrives within 24 hours, Stripe returns the exact same response without re-processing.

This is why Stripe charges can be safely retried. The retry either: gets the same result (key already processed), or processes it fresh (key not yet seen — first successful attempt). In both cases the customer is charged exactly once.

Your internal payment service should work the same way. Any non-idempotent endpoint that must be retryable should accept and honour an Idempotency-Key header.

Request Persistence — "Process It When the Service Comes Back"

From your notes: "I don't want the user to send the request again — process it whenever the service is back up." Here is exactly how to build this.

🤔 The Problem — You Cannot Drop Requests

PaymentService is down. A user checks out. You have two choices:

  • Drop the request: Tell the user "Payment failed, try again." The user is frustrated. They might not come back. You lost the sale.
  • Persist and process later: Accept the request, save it durably, tell the user "Order confirmed, processing payment." Process when the service recovers. User is happy. Sale is saved.

Option 2 is obviously better. But "save it and process later" is harder than it sounds. The naive approach has a fatal flaw called the dual-write problem.

⚙️ How the Outbox Pattern Works — Complete Explanation

The key insight is: you cannot atomically write to two different systems (DB and Kafka). But you CAN atomically write to two tables in the same database. Use this to your advantage.

Step 1 — Same transaction: In your service method, within ONE database transaction, you write the business data (the order) AND you write an outbox record (the event to be published later). If either write fails, both roll back. They are always in sync.

Step 2 — Relay process: A separate background process (a @Scheduled method, a separate thread, or a CDC tool like Debezium) reads PENDING records from the outbox table and publishes them to Kafka. It then marks them as SENT.

Step 3 — Idempotency on consumer: The relay might publish the same event twice (if it crashes after publishing but before marking as SENT). Consumers must handle duplicate events — use the event ID as an idempotency key at the consumer.

// STEP 1: Service — write order + outbox in ONE transaction @Service public class OrderService { @Transactional // one atomic unit — both writes or neither public Order placeOrder(OrderRequest request) { // Write business data Order order = orderRepository.save(new Order(request)); // Write outbox event — same transaction! OutboxEvent event = OutboxEvent.builder() .id(UUID.randomUUID().toString()) // for idempotency .aggregateType("Order") .aggregateId(order.getId().toString()) .type("ORDER_PLACED") .payload(objectMapper.writeValueAsString(order)) .status(OutboxStatus.PENDING) .createdAt(Instant.now()) .build(); outboxRepository.save(event); // Return immediately — no Kafka call here return order; } } // STEP 2: Relay — reads outbox and publishes to Kafka @Component public class OutboxRelayService { @Scheduled(fixedDelay = 1000) // run every 1 second public void relayPendingEvents() { List<OutboxEvent> pending = outboxRepository .findTop100ByStatusOrderByCreatedAtAsc(OutboxStatus.PENDING); for (OutboxEvent event : pending) { try { // Publish to Kafka — use event ID as message key for ordering kafkaTemplate.send(event.getType(), event.getId(), event.getPayload()) .get(5, TimeUnit.SECONDS); // wait for broker ack // Mark as sent only AFTER successful publish event.setStatus(OutboxStatus.SENT); event.setSentAt(Instant.now()); } catch (Exception e) { // Kafka down — increment retry count, try again next run event.incrementRetries(); if (event.getRetries() > 10) { event.setStatus(OutboxStatus.DEAD_LETTER); // alert team } log.error("Failed to relay event {}: {}", event.getId(), e.getMessage()); } outboxRepository.save(event); } } } // STEP 3: Consumer — handle duplicates with idempotency check @KafkaListener(topics = "ORDER_PLACED") public void handleOrderPlaced( @Header(KafkaHeaders.MESSAGE_KEY) String eventId, String payload, Acknowledgment ack) { // Check if we have already processed this event (duplicate detection) if (processedEventStore.exists(eventId)) { log.info("Duplicate event {} skipped", eventId); ack.acknowledge(); // ack to move offset forward return; } OrderEvent order = objectMapper.readValue(payload, OrderEvent.class); shippingService.scheduleDelivery(order); processedEventStore.markProcessed(eventId); // prevent future duplicates ack.acknowledge(); }
📋 Outbox vs Debezium vs Dead Letter Queue — Which to Use?

Polling Outbox (what we built above): A @Scheduled job polls the outbox table every 1 second. Simple to implement. Works well for low-to-medium volume (up to ~10,000 events/minute). Adds 0-1s of latency. Some DB polling overhead.

Debezium (Change Data Capture): Instead of polling, Debezium reads the database's transaction log (MySQL binlog, PostgreSQL WAL) directly. It captures changes the instant they commit — near zero latency, no polling overhead. More complex to set up (separate Debezium service). Used by LinkedIn, Shopify at high volume.

Dead Letter Queue (DLQ): Handles a different problem — messages that failed to be processed by the consumer after multiple retries. Not for persisting requests when the service is down. Use DLQ alongside the Outbox, not instead of it.

Follow-Up Questions & Full Answers

Exact questions interviewers ask after you explain resilience patterns. Click to expand.

Q1: Circuit breaker and retry both exist — which runs first? Can they conflict? +

This is an excellent question about the order of execution. In Resilience4j with stacked annotations, the execution order from outermost to innermost is: TimeLimiter → CircuitBreaker → Retry.

What this means in practice: Retry runs first on a failure. If all retries are exhausted and the call still fails, the CircuitBreaker records all those failures. When enough retries fail across enough calls, the CircuitBreaker opens.

The potential conflict: If Retry is configured to retry 3 times and CircuitBreaker uses a sliding window of 10 calls, each request that fails all 3 retries counts as 3 failures in the CircuitBreaker window, not 1. This can cause the circuit to open much faster than intended.

Best practice to avoid conflict: Keep retry count low (2–3 max) so the circuit breaker does not open prematurely. Configure the retry to only retry on truly transient errors (network exceptions, 503). Configure the circuit breaker on a larger window so random blips do not trip it. Use the circuit breaker's recordExceptions config to specify exactly which exceptions count as failures.

Think of it as: Retry handles individual transient blips. Circuit Breaker handles sustained outages. They are complementary, not competing — just be careful about how their windows interact.

Q2: How do you store idempotency keys — Redis or database? What TTL do you set? +

Redis is the preferred choice for most use cases because:

  • Sub-millisecond lookup — checking an idempotency key should add no noticeable latency
  • Built-in TTL — keys automatically expire without any manual cleanup job
  • Atomic SET with NX (set only if not exists) — race condition safe: two simultaneous requests with the same key are handled correctly
  • Memory-efficient for this use case — keys are just strings, values are compact JSON

Use a database when: You need idempotency guarantees that survive a Redis cluster failure, when the idempotency record needs to be part of an audit trail, or when you need to query idempotency records (Redis is not queryable like a DB).

TTL — how long to keep keys: The TTL should cover the maximum window in which a client might retry. Stripe uses 24 hours. For most APIs, 24 hours is appropriate — if a client is still retrying after 24 hours something is seriously wrong with their retry logic. For financial transactions, consider 7 days to account for batch reconciliation processes. Never set TTL shorter than your maximum retry duration.

Important edge case: The first request is processing and has not yet stored the result when a duplicate arrives. Use Redis SET NX (set if not exists) to reserve the key first: set key = "PROCESSING". Then process. Then set key = actual result. If a duplicate arrives while processing, it sees "PROCESSING" and either waits (poll) or returns a 409 Conflict. This prevents the window where two simultaneous first attempts both pass the "not found" check.

Q3: What happens if the Outbox relay process itself crashes mid-batch? +

This is the key reliability question for the Outbox pattern. If the relay publishes event A to Kafka and then crashes before marking it as SENT, event A will be published again on the next run. This is called at-least-once delivery.

Handling it on the consumer side: Consumers must be idempotent. Every event has a unique ID (set at outbox creation time). The consumer checks if it has already processed an event with this ID before processing. If yes, skip it. This is the processed event store we showed in the code example.

Preventing it in the relay: Process events one at a time and mark each as SENT immediately after publishing, rather than processing in large batches. This minimises the window of duplication. Using a database transaction to atomically publish to Kafka AND mark as SENT is not possible (two different systems), so at-least-once delivery is inherent — design consumers to be idempotent.

Multiple relay instances: If you run multiple relay instances for high availability, two instances might pick up the same pending event. Use optimistic locking on the outbox record (a version field) or use a SELECT FOR UPDATE to claim an event before processing it. Only one instance can claim any given event.

Q4: What is the bulkhead pattern and why is it important? +

The bulkhead pattern isolates failures by giving each downstream dependency its own resource pool (usually a thread pool) rather than sharing one pool across all downstream calls.

Without bulkheads: Your service has one thread pool of 20 threads shared across all downstream calls. InventoryService becomes slow. 20 simultaneous requests to InventoryService fill all 20 threads. Now when PaymentService calls come in — no threads available. PaymentService calls fail even though PaymentService itself is perfectly healthy. One slow dependency took down all your other dependencies.

With bulkheads: Separate thread pools: 5 threads for InventoryService calls, 10 threads for PaymentService calls, 5 threads for everything else. InventoryService fills its 5 threads. PaymentService still has its own 10 threads. PaymentService calls continue normally. The blast radius of InventoryService's slowness is contained to those 5 threads only.

In Resilience4j: Configure a Bulkhead with a max concurrent calls limit per service. Calls that exceed the limit immediately get a BulkheadFullException rather than queuing — this is fail-fast behaviour that prevents thread starvation.

Named after ship bulkheads: a cargo ship has watertight compartments. A hole in compartment 3 floods compartment 3 but the bulkheads prevent the water reaching compartments 1, 2, 4, and 5. The ship does not sink. Your service does not cascade.

Q5: How do you test circuit breakers and resilience in a lower environment? +

Testing resilience is one of the most underinvested areas in most teams. Here are the practical approaches:

Unit testing with Resilience4j test utilities: Resilience4j provides test helpers that let you manually transition the circuit breaker to any state. You can test your fallback method directly by forcing the circuit to OPEN state and verifying the correct fallback response is returned.

Integration testing with WireMock: Use WireMock to simulate downstream services. Configure it to return errors (500, timeouts, connection refused) for specific requests. Verify that your service: retries the correct number of times, opens the circuit after enough failures, returns the correct fallback, and recovers when WireMock starts returning success.

Chaos engineering: In a staging environment, use tools like Chaos Monkey for Spring Boot (part of Netflix's chaos engineering suite) to randomly kill service instances, add network latency, or exhaust resources. Verify that your system degrades gracefully rather than failing completely. Netflix does this in production — they deliberately kill production services to verify resilience. Most teams do this in staging only.

Manual testing with kubectl: In Kubernetes, you can simulate a service going down by scaling its deployment to zero: kubectl scale deployment payment-service --replicas=0. Then make requests and verify the circuit breaker opens and the fallback is returned. Then scale back up and verify the circuit recovers.

Q6: How do you monitor and alert on circuit breaker state in production? +

When a circuit breaker opens in production, it means a downstream service is failing. You need to know immediately — not when a customer complains 30 minutes later.

Resilience4j + Spring Boot Actuator: Resilience4j automatically exposes circuit breaker metrics via Spring Boot Actuator. The metrics include: current state (CLOSED/OPEN/HALF_OPEN), failure rate, slow call rate, number of calls in each outcome. These are exposed at /actuator/health and as Micrometer metrics.

Prometheus + Grafana: Micrometer publishes Resilience4j metrics to Prometheus. In Grafana, create dashboards showing: failure rate over time per circuit breaker, state transitions (each OPEN event), recovery time (how long each OPEN period lasted), and slow call rate. Set alerts to trigger when a circuit breaker transitions to OPEN.

Event listeners: Register a Spring event listener on CircuitBreakerOnStateTransitionEvent. When the circuit opens, immediately publish an alert to PagerDuty, OpsGenie, or Slack. Include: which service is down, the failure rate that triggered the trip, and the time it happened. The on-call engineer should know within 30 seconds.

What to alert on: Alert on CLOSED → OPEN transitions (a service went down). Also alert on HALF_OPEN → OPEN transitions (service is still not recovering). Consider also alerting on high failure rate even before the circuit opens — catching degradation before it becomes an outage.

Interview Tip — Answering "What Happens When a Service Goes Down?"

This is one of the most open-ended and revealing interview questions. A weak answer: "We use circuit breakers." A strong answer walks through the full strategy:

"We handle service failures in layers. First, every outbound call has a configured timeout — we never wait more than 3 seconds for a response from any service. Second, we use Resilience4j with exponential backoff and jitter for transient failures — things like brief network blips. Third, we have a circuit breaker per downstream service — after 50% of calls fail, the circuit opens and we return a pre-designed fallback response immediately rather than making doomed calls.
