Authn, Authz & Filter Chain

Authn, Authz & Filter Chain is a high-value senior interview topic because it reveals whether you understand the underlying mechanism, the default behavior, and the trade-offs in production.

🧒 ELI5

A simple mental picture

Imagine a busy workplace where one small rule determines whether the whole team moves smoothly or keeps stepping on each other. Spring Security processes requests through a filter chain that authenticates the caller, builds a security context, and then applies authorization rules. works like that kind of rule: it tells the system how to behave before small mistakes turn into bigger production problems.

It matters because security decisions must happen before business logic sees the request. Without that rule, engineers often discover the problem only after the app is under load, when the bug is expensive and confusing.

The useful mental shortcut is to ask: “What is the system trying to coordinate here, and what breaks when that coordination is missing?” That question usually reveals why this feature exists.

🔗 ANALOGY

Why the rule exists

Think of authn, authz & filter chain as a piece of operating policy, not just a syntax feature. The policy exists to make decisions repeatable across the whole system instead of relying on every individual class to remember the rule.

If you ignore that policy, the most common smell is confusing “who are you?” with “what are you allowed to do?”. In real applications, that smell usually shows up as flaky behavior, hidden coupling, or painful debugging.

This is where interviewers are listening for judgment: not just what the feature does, but how you would keep the design honest when multiple moving parts are involved.
